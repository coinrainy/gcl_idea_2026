As the official code for BGRL is not available, we implement it by ourselves, and try our best to reflect the details provided in the original paper*(Thakoor et al. [2021](#bib.bib35 \"\"))*.\nWe also report previously published results of other representative methods, such as DeepWalk *(Perozzi, Al-Rfou, and Skiena [2014](#bib.bib27 \"\"))*, DGI *(Veličković et al. [2021](#bib.bib47 \"\"))*.\n\n#### Evaluation protocol.\n\nWe evaluateAFGRLon three node-level tasks, i.e., node classification, node clustering and node similarity search.\nWe first train all models in an unsupervised manner.\nFor node classification, we use the learned embeddings to train and test a simple logistic regression classifier*(Veličković et al. [2018](#bib.bib38 \"\"))*. We report the test performance when the performance on validation data gives the best result. For node clustering and similarity search, we perform evaluations on the learned embeddings at every epoch and report the best performance.\n\n#### Implementation details.\n\nWe use a GCN*(Kipf and We have the following observations: 1) Our augmentation-freeAFGRLgenerally performs well on all datasets compared with augmentation-based methods, i.e., GRACE, GCA and BGRL, whose reported results are obtained by carefully tuning the augmentation hyperparameters. Recall that in Table[1](#S5.T1 \"Table 1 ‣ 5 Proposed Method ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\") we demonstrated that their performance is highly sensitive to the choice of augmentation hyperparameters. This verifies the benefit of our augmentation-free approach. 2) We also evaluateAFGRLon node clustering (Table[3](#S6.T3 \"Table 3 ‣ Implementation details. ‣ 6.1 Experimental Setup ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\")) and similarity search (Table[4](#S6.T4 \"Table 4 ‣ Implementation details. ‣ 6.1 Experimental Setup ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\")).\nNote that the best hyperparameters for node classification task were adopted.\nTable [3](#S6.T3 \"Table 3 ‣ Implementation details. ‣ 6.1 Experimental Setup ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\") shows thatAFGRLgenerally outperforms other methods in node clustering task. We argue that this is mainly becauseAFGRLalso considers global semantic information unlike the compared methods. 3) It is worth noting that methods built upon instance discrimination principle*(Wu et al. [2018](#bib.bib39 \"\"))*, i.e., GRACE and GCA, are not only memory consuming (OOM on large datasets), but also generally perform worse than their counterparts on various tasks (especially on clustering). This indicates that instance discrimination, which treats all other nodes except itself as negatives without considering the graph structural information, is not appropriate for graph-structured data, especially for clustering in which the global structural information is crucial. 4) AFGRLgenerally performs well on node similarity search (Table[4](#S6.T4 \"Table 4 ‣ Implementation details. ‣ 6.1 Experimental Setup ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\")). This is expected becauseAFGRLaims to make nearest neighbors of each node share the same label with the query node by discovering the local and the global positives.\n\n<img src='x6.png' alt='Refer to caption' title='' width='428' height='126' />\n\n*Figure 6: Ablation study onAFGRL.*\n\n#### Ablation Studies.\n\nTo verify the benefit of each component ofAFGRL, we conduct ablation studies on two datasets that exhibit distinct characteristics, i.e., Amazon Computers (E-commerce network) and WikiCS (Reference network). In Figure[6](#S6.F6 \"Figure 6 ‣ Overall evaluation. ‣ 6.2 Performance Analysis ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\"), we observe that considering both local structural and global semantic information shows the best performance. Moreover, we observe that the global semantic information is more beneficial than the local structural information. This can be explained by the performance of “$k$-NN only” variant, which performs on par with “$k$-NN + Adj” variant. That is, we conjecture that performing $k$-NN on the node representations learned by our framework can capture sufficient local structural information contained in the adjacency matrix. Based on the ablation studies, we argue thatAFGRLstill gives competitive performance even when the adjacency matrix is sparse, which shows the practicality of our proposed framework. Finally, the low performance of “Clus-only” variant implies the importance of considering the local structural information in graph-structured data.\n\n<img src='x7.png' alt='Refer to caption' title='' width='428' height='160' />\n\n*Figure 7: Effect of embedding dimension size ($D$).*\n\n#### Hyperparameter Analysis.\n\nFigure[5](#S5.F5 \"Figure 5 ‣ Table 2 ‣ 5.1 Augmentation-Free GRL (AFGRL) ‣ 5 Proposed Method ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\") shows the sensitivity analysis on the hyperparameters $k$ and $M$ ofAFGRL. We observe that $k\\=4$ and $M>1$ generally give the best performance, while the performance is rather stable over various $M$s.\nThis verifies that our augmentation-free approach can be easily trained compared with other augmentation-based methods, i.e., stable over hyperparameters, while outperforming them in most cases.\nMoreover, in Figure [7](#S6.F7 \"Figure 7 ‣ Ablation Studies. ‣ 6.2 Performance Analysis ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\"), we conduct experiments across various sizes of node embedding dimension $D$. We observe thatAFGRLbenefits from high-dimensional embeddings, while other methods rapidly saturate when the dimension of embeddings increase. Note that*Zbontar et al. ([2021](#bib.bib43 \"\"))* recently showed similar results indicating that methods based on instance discrimination*(Wu et al. [2018](#bib.bib39 \"\"))* is prone to the curse of dimensionality.\n\n<img src='x8.png' alt='Refer to caption' title='' width='387' height='342' />\n\n*Figure 8: t-SNE embeddings of nodes in Photo dataset.*\n\n#### Visualization of embeddings.\n\nTo provide a more intuitive understanding of the learned node embeddings, we visualize node embeddings of GCA (Figure[8](#S6.F8 \"Figure 8 ‣ Hyperparameter Analysis. ‣ 6.2 Performance Analysis ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\")(a)) andAFGRL(Figure[8](#S6.F8 \"Figure 8 ‣ Hyperparameter Analysis. ‣ 6.2 Performance Analysis ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\")(b)) by using t-SNE*(Van der Maaten and Hinton [2008](#bib.bib36 \"\"))*. Each point represents a node, and the color represents the node label. We observe that node embeddings generated by both methods are grouped together according to their corresponding node labels.\nHowever, the major difference is thatAFGRLcaptures more fine-grained class information compared with GCA. That is, forAFGRL, there tend to be small clusters within each label group.\nTo emphasize this, we sample the same set of nodes from each label, and compare their embeddings (Figure[8](#S6.F8 \"Figure 8 ‣ Hyperparameter Analysis. ‣ 6.2 Performance Analysis ‣ 6 Experiments ‣ Augmentation-Free Self-Supervised Learning on Graphs\") bottom).\nWe clearly observe that nodes are more tightly grouped inAFGRLcompared with GCA, which implies thatAFGRLcaptures more fine-grained class information."
