[1](#S5.T1 \"Table 1 ‣ 5.1 Experimental Settings ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Revisiting Positive Samples in Graph Contrastive Learning: From the Perspective of Message Passing\"),\nSPGCL achieves the best accuracy on all six homophilic datasets.\nThe gain mainly comes from separate propagation, which removes the trivial positive similarity introduced by message passing and makes positive pairs provide non-trivial learning signals.\nFirst, compared with methods that learn only use positive samples, such as BGRL and AFGRL, SPGCL outperforms them significantly.\nAlthough these methods avoid explicit negative sampling through bootstrap-style training strategy, they still operate on representations produced by standard GCN propagation, where message passing induces a pre-alignment effect that trivializes positive similarity.\nAs a result, the learning signal extracted from positive pairs remains limited, whereas SPGCL explicitly mitigates this issue through energy-aware feature propagation.\nMoreover, SPGCL outperforms methods that improve data augmentation or positive sampling strategies, including GCA, LocalGCL, and CSGCL.\nThese methods strengthen positive construction through\nimproved perturbations or sampling rules, but they still build positives on embeddings that have already been partly aligned by message passing.\nThis limits the effective learning signals from positive samples.\nFinally, methods that focus on negative sample optimization, such as ProGCL and SGRL, achieve competitive but consistently inferior performance compared to SPGCL.\nThis suggests that refining negative samples alone is insufficient to exploit the full potential of contrastive learning, and that effectively restoring informative positive learning plays a more critical role in representation quality.\n\nNode classification on heterophilic datasets.\n\nTable 2: Node clustering performance evaluated by NMI and Homogeneity. heterophilic graphs, and the results are reported in Table [3](#S5.T3 \"Table 3 ‣ 5.2 Performance Analysis ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Revisiting Positive Samples in Graph Contrastive Learning: From the Perspective of Message Passing\").\nCompared with methods designed for homophilic graphs, including GRACE, DGI, BGRL, GCA, and LocalGCL, SPGCL achieves significant performance improvements across all datasets.\nThis gap mainly arises from the positive sampling strategies\n(augmentation-based or neighbor-based) adopted by these methods.\nAugmentation-based methods construct positive samples by perturbing node features or graph structures, which destroy core semantic information in\nheterophilic graphs, leading to unreliable positives.\nNeighbor-based positive sampling, which works well under the homophily assumption, becomes ineffective in heterophilic settings, as adjacent nodes often belong to different classes and enforcing their alignment introduces misleading signals.\nMoreover, SPGCL also outperforms methods designed for heterophilic graphs.\nAlthough these methods introduce different designs to handle heterophily, such as modeling higher-order dependencies (Chen et al., [2022](#bib.bib28 \"Towards self-supervised learning on graphs with heterophily\")), latent semantics (Xiao et al., [2022](#bib.bib14 \"Decoupled self-supervised learning for graphs\")), or asymmetric neighborhoods (Xiao et al., [2023](#bib.bib25 \"Simple and asymmetric graph contrastive learning without augmentations\")),\nthey all operate on representations where all feature dimensions are uniformly propagated through the graph.\nAs a result, positive samples are aligned without distinguishing which feature dimensions contribute to their similarity.\nIn contrast, SPGCL explicitly separates feature propagation based on Dirichlet energy, which introduces feature-level control over how positive similarity is constructed.\n\nTable 3: Node classification results on Hyper-parameter analysis of SPGCL with respect to the $\\operatorname{TopK}$ threshold. We report the classification performance on both homophilic (left) and heterophilic (right) graphs.\n\nTable [4](#S5.T4 \"Table 4 ‣ 5.2 Performance Analysis ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Revisiting Positive Samples in Graph Contrastive Learning: From the Perspective of Message Passing\") reports the ablation results on homophilic and heterophilic datasets. Removing either Energy-Aware Propagation (EAP) or Energy-guided Positive Sampling (EPS) consistently degrades performance, and the drop is particularly significant when EAP is removed. This confirms that separating feature propagation based on Dirichlet energy is the core factor for restoring effective positive learning.\nWe also conduct hyper-parameter experiments.\nFigure [3](#S5.F3 \"Figure 3 ‣ 5.3 Model Analysis ‣ 5 Experiments ‣ Revisiting Positive Samples in Graph Contrastive Learning: From the Perspective of Message Passing\") analyzes the impact of the $\\operatorname{TopK}$ used to select high-energy feature dimensions. On homophilic graphs, performance remains relatively stable as $\\operatorname{TopK}$ increases, with a slight downward trend. This reflects a trade-off between two effects: expanding the high-energy subset weakens the selectivity of feature propagation for positive alignment, while simultaneously enlarging the low-energy subset used for positive sampling, which yields more stable positive pairs. These two effects partially offset each other, resulting in overall stable performance.\nIn contrast, on heterophilic graphs, performance exhibits a clear decreasing trend as $\\operatorname{TopK}$ increases. In this setting, propagating a broader set of features amplifies the mixing of semantically dissimilar neighbors, causing positive pairs from different classes to become overly similar. Although low-energy features can still identify relatively reliable positives, the degradation of positive learning signals induced by propagation dominates, leading to a more pronounced performance drop."
