  "content": "Graph Representation Learning.\nGraph Representation Learning (GRL) aims to embed high-dimensional, sparse, and non-Euclidean graph-structured data into low-dimensional representations for various downstream tasks, such as node classification, clustering, and link prediction (Chen et al., [2020](#bib.bib26 \"Graph representation learning: a survey\")).\nEarly GRL methods primarily rely on matrix factorization (Zhang, [2022](#bib.bib34 \"An introduction to matrix factorization and factorization machines in recommendation system, and beyond\")) or random walk–based techniques (Lawler and Limic, [2010](#bib.bib41 \"Random walk: a modern introduction\")), which focus on either graph structure or node attributes and thus struggle to jointly model both (Ribeiro et al., [2017](#bib.bib46 \"Struc2vec: learning node representations from structural identity\")).\nWith the emergence of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), such as GCN (Kipf and Welling, [2017](#bib.bib20 \"Semi-supervised classification with graph convolutional networks\")), GAT (Velickovic et al., [2018](#bib.bib16 \"Graph attention networks\")), and GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., [2017](#bib.bib17 \"Inductive representation learning on large graphs\")), message passing and aggregation mechanism makes it possible to jointly embed graph structure and node attributes in representations.\nDespite their success, GNNs typically require large amounts of labeled data for supervision, which is often expensive and impractical in real-world (Liu et al., [2022b](#bib.bib27 \"Graph self-supervised learning: a survey\")).\nTo address this limitation, Graph Contrastive Learning (GCL) has emerged as a mainstream self-supervised pre-training paradigm, which trains graph encoders by maximizing the similarity between positive samples and minimizing it between negative samples without labels.\n\nGraph Contrastive Learning.\nIn GCLs, it is a consensus that positive samples play a key role, as contrastive objectives rely on aligning semantically consistent views of the same node or subgraph to learn invariant representations (Zhu et al., [2020](#bib.bib23 \"Deep graph contrastive representation learning\"); Velickovic et al., [2019](#bib.bib12 \"Deep graph infomax\"); Thakoor et al., [2021](#bib.bib4 \"Bootstrapped representation learning on graphs\"); Liu et al., [2022a](#bib.bib45 \"Revisiting graph contrastive learning from the perspective of graph spectrum\"); Zhuo et al., [2024a](#bib.bib58 \"Unified graph augmentations for generalized contrastive learning on graphs\")).\nGCLs typically construct positive samples in two ways: (i) the same anchor node across multiple views generated by data augmentations, and (ii) its local neighbors sampled from graphs.\nConsequently, many existing methods focus on refining positive samples through improved data augmentation and neighborhood-based positive sampling.\nEarly GCLs adopt random feature masking and edge dropping to generate positive views (Zhu et al., [2020](#bib.bib23 \"Deep graph contrastive representation learning\"); Velickovic et al., [2019](#bib.bib12 \"Deep graph infomax\"); Thakoor et al., [2021](#bib.bib4 \"Bootstrapped representation learning on graphs\"); Zhuo et al., [2024b](#bib.bib57 \"Improving graph contrastive learning via adaptive positive sampling\")).\nHowever, such random perturbations may destroy critical structural and semantic information, leading to misleading positive pairs.\nTo address this, some methods design augmentation strategies based on graph properties.\nGCA (Zhu et al., [2021](#bib.bib18 \"Graph contrastive learning with adaptive augmentation\")) and GRADE (Wang et al., [2022](#bib.bib24 \"Uncovering the structural fairness in graph contrastive learning\")) improve data augmentation according to node centrality or degree, reducing the risk that positive samples become semantically inconsistent due to excessive structural corruption.\nCSGCL (Chen et al., [2023](#bib.bib10 \"CSGCL: community-strength-enhanced graph contrastive learning\")) incorporates community structure into augmentation design, encouraging positive samples to preserve consistent community-level semantics.\nCOSTA (Zhang et al., [2022b](#bib.bib9 \"COSTA: covariance-preserving feature augmentation for graph contrastive learning\")) constrains feature perturbations by preserving feature covariance, so that positive samples constructed from different views retain consistent attribute semantics.\nMoreover, some methods improve data augmentation from a spectral perspective to enhance the quality of positive samples.\nSpCo (Liu et al., [2022a](#bib.bib45 \"Revisiting graph contrastive learning from the perspective of graph spectrum\")) reveals that effective augmentations should preserve low-frequency components while perturbing high-frequency ones.\nIt perturbs edges with high-frequency while retaining low-frequency edges during augmentations.\nHowever, these methods rely on manually designed rules, which limits their adaptability across diverse graphs.\nTo address this, AD-GCL (Suresh et al., [2021](#bib.bib1 \"Adversarial graph augmentation to improve graph contrastive learning\")) trains a neural network to generate diverse views by minimizing mutual information between original and augmented graphs, improving the construction of informative positive samples without predefined heuristics augmentation. Notably, BGRL (Thakoor et al., [2021](#bib.bib4 \"Bootstrapped representation learning on graphs\")) and AFGRL (Lee et al., [2022](#bib.bib3 \"Augmentation-free self-supervised learning on graphs\")) adopt a bootstrap framework that trains models without negative samples.\nThey employ two asymmetric graph encoders and learn representations by aligning positive pairs across different views.\nThese methods represent an early attempt to exploit positive samples learning in GCLs.\nHowever, recent studies show that the effectiveness of such bootstrap frameworks is highly sensitive to batch normalization, which implicitly introduces negative sample information (Fetterman and Albrecht, [2020](#bib.bib51 \"Understanding self-supervised and contrastive learning with bootstrap your own latent (byol)\"); He et al., [2024a](#bib.bib42 \"Exploitation of a latent mechanism in graph contrastive learning: representation scattering\")).\nConsequently, the utilization of positive samples in these methods remains limited.\n\nIn contrast to prior work that mainly focuses on constructing better positive samples, we revisit how positive samples are actually utilized in GCLs.\nWe show that message passing induces a pre-alignment effect, which increases positive similarity before contrastive optimization and consequently weakens the effective learning signal from positive samples.\nThis perspective departs from existing augmentation and sampling strategies and motivates a novel design to restore informative positive learning in GCLs."
