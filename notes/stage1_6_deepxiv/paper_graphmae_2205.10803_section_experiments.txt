  "content": "In this section, we demonstrate that GraphMAE is a general self-supervised framework for various graph learning tasks, including:\n\n* •\n\n    Unsupervised representation learning for node classification;\n\n* •\n\n    Unsupervised representation learning for graph classification;\n\n* •\n\n    Transfer learning on molecular property prediction.\n\nExtensive experiments on various datasets are conducted to evaluate\nthe performance of GraphMAE against state-of-the-art (SOTA) contrastive and generative methods on these three tasks.\nIn each task, we follow exactly the same experimental procedure, e.g., data splits, evaluation protocol, as the standard settings*(Veličković et al., [2018](#bib.bib41 \"\"); Zhang\net al., [2021](#bib.bib58 \"\"); Sun\net al., [2019](#bib.bib35 \"\"); Hu et al., [2020c](#bib.bib17 \"\"))*.\n\n### 4.1. Node Classification\n\nSetup. The node classification task is to predict the unknown node labels in networks.\nWe test the performance of GraphMAE on 6 standard benchmarks: Cora, Citeseer, PubMed*(Yang\net al., [2016](#bib.bib49 \"\"))*, Ogbn-arxiv*(Hu et al., [2020b](#bib.bib16 \"\"))*, PPI, and Reddit.\nFollowing the inductive setup in GraphSage*(Hamilton\net al., [2017](#bib.bib11 \"\"))*, the testing for Reddit and PPI is carried out on unseen nodes and graphs, while the other networks are used for transductive learning.\n\nFor the evaluation protocol, we follow the experimental setting in*(Veličković et al., [2018](#bib.bib41 \"\"); Hassani and\nKhasahmadi, [2020](#bib.bib12 \"\"); Thakoor et al., [2022](#bib.bib38 \"\"); Zhang\net al., [2021](#bib.bib58 \"\"))*.\nFirst, we train a GNN encoder by the proposed GraphMAE without supervision.\nThen we freeze the parameters of the encoder and generate all the nodes’ embeddings.\nFor evaluation, we train a linear classifier and report the mean accuracy on the test nodes through 20 random initializations.\nWe follow the public data splits*(Veličković et al., [2018](#bib.bib41 \"\"); Hassani and\nKhasahmadi, [2020](#bib.bib12 \"\"); Zhang\net al., [2021](#bib.bib58 \"\"))* of Cora, Citeseer, and PubMed.\nThe graph encoder $f_{E}$ and decoder $f_{D}$ are both specified as standard GAT.\nDetailed hyper-parameters can be found in Appendix [A](#A1 \"Appendix A Appendix ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\").\n\nResults. We compare GraphMAE with SOTA contrastive self-supervised models, DGI*(Veličković et al., [2018](#bib.bib41 \"\"))*, MVGRL*(Hassani and\nKhasahmadi, [2020](#bib.bib12 \"\"))*, GRACE*(Zhu\net al., [2020b](#bib.bib61 \"\"))*, BGRL*(Thakoor et al., [2022](#bib.bib38 \"\"))*, InfoGCL*(Xu\net al., [2021a](#bib.bib45 \"\"))*, and CCA-SSG*(Zhang\net al., [2021](#bib.bib58 \"\"))*, as well as supervised baselines GCN and GAT.\nWe also report the results of previous generative self-supervised models, GAE*(Kipf and Welling, [2016](#bib.bib21 \"\"))*, GPT-GNN*(Hu\net al., [2020a](#bib.bib18 \"\"))*, and GATE*(Salehi and\nDavulcu, [2020](#bib.bib32 \"\"))*.\nWe report results from previous works with the same experimental setup if available.\nIf results are not previously reported and codes are provided, we implement them based on the official codes and conduct a hyper-parameter search.\nTable[1](#S4.T1 \"Table 1 ‣ 4.2. Graph Classification ‣ 4. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\") lists the results.\nGraphMAE achieves the best or competitive results compared to the SOTA self-supervised approaches in all benchmarks.\nNotably, GraphMAE outperforms existing generative methods by a large margin.\nThe results in the inductive setting of PPI and Reddit suggest the self-supervised GraphMAE technique provides strong generalization to unseen nodes.\n\n### 4.2. Graph Classification\n\nSetup. For graph classification, we conduct experiments on 7 benchmarks: MUTAG, IMDB-B, IMDB-M, PROTEINS, COLLAB, REDDIT-B, and NCI1*(Yanardag and\nVishwanathan, [2015](#bib.bib48 \"\"))*.\nEach dataset is a collection of graphs where each graph is associated with a label.\nNode labels are used as input features in MUTAG, PROTEINS, and NCI1, whereas node degrees are used in IMDB-B, IMDB-M, REDDIT-B, and COLLAB.\n\nFor the evaluation protocol, after generating graph embeddings with GraphMAE’s encoder and readout function, we feed encoded graph-level representations into a downstream LIBSVM*(Chang and Lin, [2011](#bib.bib3 \"\"))* classifier to predict the label, and report the mean 10-fold cross-validation accuracy with standard deviation after 5 runs. We adopt GIN*(Xu\net al., [2019](#bib.bib46 \"\"))*, which is commonly used in previous graph classification works, as the backbone of encoder and decoder.\n\nResults. In addition to classical graph kernel methods—Weisfeiler-Lehman sub-tree kernel (WL)*(Shervashidze et al., [2011](#bib.bib33 \"\"))* and deep graph kernel (DGK)*(Yanardag and\nVishwanathan, [2015](#bib.bib48 \"\"))*, we also compare GraphMAE with SOTA unsupervised and contrastive methods,\nGCC*(Qiu et al., [2020](#bib.bib30 \"\"))*, graph2vec*(Narayanan et al., [2017](#bib.bib26 \"\"))*, Infograph*(Sun\net al., [2019](#bib.bib35 \"\"))*, GraphCL*(You\net al., [2020a](#bib.bib55 \"\"))*, JOAO*(You\net al., [2021](#bib.bib54 \"\"))*, MVGRL*(Hassani and\nKhasahmadi, [2020](#bib.bib12 \"\"))*, and InfoGCL*(Xu\net al., [2021a](#bib.bib45 \"\"))*.\nThe supervised baselines, GIN*(Xu\net al., [2019](#bib.bib46 \"\"))* and DiffPool*(Ying et al., [2018](#bib.bib51 \"\"))*, are also included.\nPer graph classification research tradition, we report results from previous papers if available.\nThe results are shown in Table[2](#S4.T2 \"Table 2 ‣ 4.2. Graph Classification ‣ 4. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\").\nWe find that GraphMAE outperforms all self-supervised baselines on five out of seven datasets and has competitive results on the other two.\nThe node features of these seven datasets are all one-hot vectors representing node-labels or degrees, which are considered to be less informative than node features in node classification. The results manifest that generative auto-encoders could learn meaningful information and offer potentials in graph-level tasks.\n\n*Table 1. Transfer Learning\n\nSetup. To evaluate the transferability of the proposed method, we test the performance on transfer learning on molecular property prediction, following the setting of*(Hu et al., [2020c](#bib.bib17 \"\"); You\net al., [2020a](#bib.bib55 \"\"), [2021](#bib.bib54 \"\"))*. The model is first pre-trained in 2 million unlabeled molecules sampled from the ZINC15*(Sterling and\nIrwin, [2015](#bib.bib34 \"\"))*, and then finetuned in 8 classification benchmark datasets contained in MoleculeNet*(Wu et al., [2018](#bib.bib44 \"\"))*. The downstream datasets are split by scaffold-split to mimic real-world use cases. Input node features are the atom number and chirality tag, and edge features are the bond type and direction.\n\nFor the evaluation protocol, we run experiments for 10 times and report the mean and standard deviation of ROC-AUC scores (%). Following the default setting in*(Hu et al., [2020c](#bib.bib17 \"\"))*, we adopt a 5-layer GIN as the encoder and a single-layer GIN as the decoder.\n\nResults. We evaluate GraphMAE against methods including Infomax, AttrMasking and ContextPred*(Hu et al., [2020c](#bib.bib17 \"\"))* , and SOTA contrastive learning methods, GraphCL*(You\net al., [2020a](#bib.bib55 \"\"))*, JOAO*(You\net al., [2021](#bib.bib54 \"\"))*, and GraphLoG*(Xu\net al., [2021b](#bib.bib47 \"\"))*. Table [2](#S4.T2 \"Table 2 ‣ 4.2. Graph Classification ‣ 4. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\") shows that the performance on downstream tasks is comparable to SOTA methods, in which GraphMAE achieves the best average scores and has a small edge over previous best results in two tasks.\nThis demonstrates the robust transferability of GraphMAE.\n\nTo summarize, the self-supervised GraphMAE method achieves competitive performance on node classification, graph classification, and transfer learning across 21 benchmarks. Note that we do not customize a dedicated GraphMAE for each task.\nThe consistent results on the three tasks demonstrate that GraphMAE is an effective and universal self-supervised graph pre-training framework for various applications.\n\n### 4.4. Ablation Studies\n\nTo verify the effects of the main components in GraphMAE, we further conduct several ablation studies. Without loss of generalization, we choose three datasets from node classification and two datasets from graph classification for experiments.\n\nEffect of reconstruction criterion. We study the influence of reconstruction criterion, and Table [4](#S4.T4 \"Table 4 ‣ 4.4. Ablation Studies ‣ 4. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\") shows the results of MSE and the SCE loss function.\nGenerally, the input features in node classification lie in continuous or high-dimensional discrete space, containing more discriminative information.\nThe results manifest that SCE has a significant advantage over MSE,\nwith an absolute gain of 1.5% $\\sim$ 8.0%.\nIn graph classification, pre-training with either MSE or SCE improves accuracy.\nThe input features are discrete one-hot encoding in these benchmarks,\nrepresenting either node degrees or node labels.\nReconstructing one-hot encoding with MSE is\nsimilar to classification tasks, thus MSE also works. Nevertheless, SCE offers a performance edge (though limited) over MSE.\n\nFigure [3](#S4.F3 \"Figure 3 ‣ 4.4. Ablation Studies ‣ 4. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\") shows the influence of scaling factor $\\gamma$. We observe that $\\gamma>1$ offers benefits in most cases, especially in node classification. However, in MUTAG, a larger $\\gamma$ value harms the performance. In our experiments, we notice that the training loss in node classification is much higher than that in graph classification.\nThis further demonstrates that aligning continuous vectors in a unit sphere is more challenging. Therefore, scaling $\\gamma$ brings improvements.\n\n*Table 4. Masking plays an important role in the proposed GraphMAE method. GraphMAE employs two masking strategies — masking input feature before encoder, and re-masking encoded code before decoder. Table [4](#S4.T4 \"Table 4 ‣ 4.4. Ablation Studies ‣ 4. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\") studies the designs. We observe a significant drop in performance if not masking input features, indicating that masking inputs is vital to avoid the trivial solution. For the re-mask strategy, the accuracy drops by 0.1%$\\sim$1.9% without it.\nRe-mask is designed for the GNN decoder and can be regarded as regularization, which makes the self-supervised task more challenging.\n\n<img src='x3.png' alt='Refer to caption' title='' width='461' height='307' />\n\n<img src='x4.png' alt='Refer to caption' title='' width='461' height='307' />\n\n*Figure 3. Ablation studies of mask ratio and scaling factor.*\n\nEffect of mask ratio. Figure [3](#S4.F3 \"Figure 3 ‣ 4.4. Ablation Studies ‣ 4. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\") shows the influence of mask ratio.\nIn most cases, the reconstruction task with a low mask ratio (0.1) is not challenging enough to learn useful features.\nThe optimal ratio varies across graphs.\nResults on Ogbn-arxiv and IMDB-B can be found in Appendix [A](#A1 \"Appendix A Appendix ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\").\nIn Cora, increasing the mask ratio by more than 0.5 degrades the performance, while GraphMAE still works with a surprisingly high ratio (0.7$\\sim$0.9) in PubMed and MUTAG.\nThis can be connected with the information redundancy in graphs.\nLarge node degrees or high homogeneity may lead to heavy information redundancy, in which missing node features may be recovered from very few neighboring nodes with little high-level understanding of\nfeatures and local context.\nIn contrast, lower redundancy means that an excessively high mask ratio would make it impossible to recover features, thus degrading the performance.\n\nEffect of decoder type. In Table [4](#S4.T4 \"Table 4 ‣ 4.4. Ablation Studies ‣ 4. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE: Self-Supervised Masked Graph Autoencoders\"), we compare different decoder types, including MLP, GCN, GIN, and GAT. The re-mask strategy is only used for GNN decoders. As the results show, using GNN decoder typically boosts the performance. Compared to MLP, which reconstructs original features from latent representations, GNN enforces masked nodes to extract information relevant to their original features from the neighborhood. One reasonable assumption is that GNN avoids the representation tending to be the same as original features.\nMLP also works in GraphMAE, which might be partly attribute to the usage of the SCE metric.\n\nAmong different GNNs, GIN performs better in graph-level tasks and GAT is a more reasonable option for node classification. It is observed that replacing GAT with GCN causes a significant drop, especially in Cora ($\\sim$2.9%) and PubMed ($\\sim$2.0%).\nWe speculate that the attention mechanism matters in reconstructing continuous features with the re-mask strategy."
