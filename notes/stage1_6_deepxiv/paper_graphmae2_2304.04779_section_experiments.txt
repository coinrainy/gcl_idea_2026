We compare GraphMAE2 with state-of-the-art self-supervised graph learning methods, including contrastive methods, GRACE*(Zhu et al., [2020](#bib.bib58 \"\"))*, BGRL*(Thakoor et al., [2022](#bib.bib40 \"\"))*, CCA-SSG*(Zhang et al., [2021](#bib.bib55 \"\"))*, and GGD*(Zheng et al., [2022](#bib.bib56 \"\"))* as well as a generative method GraphMAE*(Hou et al., [2022](#bib.bib19 \"\"))*.\nOther methods are not compared because they are not scalable to large graphs, e.g., MVGRL*(Hassani and Khasahmadi, [2020](#bib.bib15 \"\"))*, or the source code has not been released, e.g., InfoGCL*(Xu et al., [2021](#bib.bib49 \"\"))*.\nAs stated in*(Trivedi et al., [2022](#bib.bib41 \"\"))*, random models can have a strong inductive bias on graphs and are non-trivial baselines. Therefore, we also report the results of the randomly-initialized GNN model and Simplified Graph Convolution (SGC)*(Wu et al., [2019](#bib.bib47 \"\"))*, which simply stacks the propagated features of different orders, to examine whether the SSL learns a more effective propagation paradigm. Comparing with them can reflect the contributions of self-supervised learning. To extend to large graphs for baselines, we adopt GraphSAINT*(Zeng et al., [2020](#bib.bib54 \"\"))* sampling strategy, which is proved to perform better than widely-adopted Neighborhood Sampling*(Hamilton et al., [2017](#bib.bib14 \"\"))* in many cases. GraphMAE and GraphMAE2 are trained based on the presented local clustering algorithm. For all baselines, we employ Graph Attention Network (GAT)*(Velickovic et al., [2018](#bib.bib42 \"\"))* as the backbone of the encoder $f_{E}$ and the decoder for input feature reconstruction $f_{D}$.\n\nEvaluation. We evaluate our approach with two setups: (i) linear probing and (ii) fine-tuning.\nFor linear probing, we first generate node embeddings with the pretrained encoder. Then we discard the encoder and train a linear classifier using the embeddings under the supervised setting.\nFor fine-tuning, we add a linear classifier on top of node representations and fine-tune all parameters under the semi-supervised setting.\nWe randomly sample 1% and 5% labels from the training set to finetune the pretrained model, aiming to test the ability to transfer knowledge learned from unlabeled data to facilitate the downstream performance with a few labels.\nFor both cases, we run the experiments for 10 trials with random seeds and report the average accuracy and standard variance.\n\nResults. The results of linear probing are illustrated in Table[3](#S3.T3 \"Table 3 ‣ 3. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE2: A Decoding-enhanced Masked Self-supervised Graph Learner\"). And we interpret the result from 3 aspects. First, GraphMAE2 achieves better results than all self-supervised baselines across all datasets. This manifests that the proposed method can learn more discriminative representations under the unsupervised setting. Notably, GraphMAE2 improves upon GraphMAE by a margin of 1.91% and 2.35% (absolute difference) on MAG-Scholar-F and Papers100M. These results demonstrate the significance of the proposed improvement.\nSecond, our approach, together with most baselines, outperforms the randomly initialized, untrained model by a large margin. This demonstrates that the designed self-supervised pretext task guides the model to better capture the semantic and structural information than the untrained model. As a comparison, improper self-supervised signals can lead the model to perform even worse, yet this phenomenon is ignored in most previous studies.\nThird, GraphMAE2 consistently generates better performance than SGC. Despite the fact that methods based on decoupled propagation have achieved promising results in the full-supervised setting with the assistance of self-training,\nwe demonstrate that graph neural networks, like GAT, could still be more powerful at generating node representations in the unsupervised setting.\n\nTable[4](#S3.T4 \"Table 4 ‣ 3.1. Evaluating on Large-scale Datasets ‣ 3. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE2: A Decoding-enhanced Masked Self-supervised Graph Learner\") shows the results of finetuning the pretrained model in the semi-supervised setting. On the one hand, it is observed that self-supervised pretraining of GraphMAE2 benefits downstream supervised training with significant performance gains. In ogbn-Products, with the pre-trained model, the performance achieves improvement by above 5.1%.\nAnd finetuning with only 5% of data generates comparative performance (80.52%) to many supervised learning methods in OGB leaderboard222<https://ogb.stanford.edu/docs/leader_nodeprop/> with all 100% of training data, e.g., GraphSAINT: 80.27, Cluster-GAT: 79.23%.\nOn the other hand, our approach remarkably achieves state-of-the-art performance for all benchmarks. The only exception is on the Products dataset with 1% training data, where GraphMAE2 slightly underperforms GRACE yet still achieves the second-best result.\nIt should be noted that in ogbn-Papers100M, only GraphMAE2 and GraphMAE generate better performance than the random-initialized model, while all contrastive baselines fail to bring improvement with pretraining. One possible reason is that the data augmentation techniques used in baselines fail in this dataset.\n\n### 3.2. Evaluating on Small-scale Datasets\n\nExperimental setup. Ablation Studies\n\nWe further conduct ablation studies to verify the contributions of the designs in GraphMAE2.\nWe choose linear probing for evaluation.\n\nAblation on the learning framework. We study the influence of the proposed two strategies—latent representation prediction and multi-view random re-masking. The results are shown in Table[6](#S3.T6 \"Table 6 ‣ 3.2. Evaluating on Small-scale Datasets ‣ 3. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE2: A Decoding-enhanced Masked Self-supervised Graph Learner\") , where the “w/o random re-mask” represents that we adopt the fixed re-masking strategy as GraphMAE.\nIt is observed that the two strategies both contribute to performance improvement.\nThis demonstrates the effectiveness and further supports our motivation for the effects of input feature quality.\nLatent representation prediction brings more benefits as the accuracy drops more when the component is removed, e.g., -1.58% in ogbn-Products and -1.91% in ogbn-Papers100M, than the multi-view random re-masking, e.g., -0.45% and -0.73%.\nThe target generator network provides valuable guidance and constraints on the encoded representation.\n\nWe also conduct an experiment by totally removing the input feature reconstruction, and the training only involves latent representation prediction.\nIn such cases, the learning degrades to self-knowledge distillation without heavy data augmentation and causes a significant drop in performance.\nThe network may fall into a trivial solution and may learn collapsed representation as the results are worse than GraphMAE or even worse than the random-initialized model in MAG-Scholar-F and ogbn-Papers100M.\nThis indicates that feature reconstruction substantially supports SSL, and the proposed two strategies serve as auxiliaries to help overcome the deficiency.\nOverall speaking, the results confirm that the superior performance of GraphMAE2 comes from the design rather than any individual contribution.\n\nAblation on sampling strategies. Table[7](#S3.T7 \"Table 7 ‣ 3.2. Evaluating on Small-scale Datasets ‣ 3. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE2: A Decoding-enhanced Masked Self-supervised Graph Learner\") shows the influence of different sampling strategies.\nWe compare local clustering against two popular subgraph sampling algorithms–ClusterGCN*(Chiang et al., [2019](#bib.bib8 \"\"))* and GraphSAINT*(Zeng et al., [2020](#bib.bib54 \"\"))*. Neighborhood sampling is not included since\nit is not friendly to masked feature reconstruction, especially with GNN decoder. The local clustering is conducive to the excellent performance of GraphMAE2, as our algorithm shows an advantage over GraphSAINT and Cluster-GCN with 0.57% and 1.49% improvement on average. Recall that GraphSAINT tends to sample nodes globally, and thus the subgraph is more sparse.\nAlthough ClusterGCN generates large and connected partitions, it suffers from high information loss as edges between clusters are abandoned.\nAnd the results indicate that the densely-connected local subgraph produced can generate better representations.\nIn addition, we compare our approach with the strongest baselines using the same sampling strategy. And GraphMAE2 still generates a 1.49% advantage in ogbn-Products, demonstrating its effectiveness.\n\nAblation on model capacity. The effects of model capacity have attracted significant attention in other fields like CV*(He et al., [2022](#bib.bib16 \"\"))* and NLP*(Brown et al., [2020](#bib.bib5 \"\"))* as it is demonstrated that SSL can largely benefit from increasing model parameters.\nWe take an interest in whether the scaling law of model capacity also applies to GNNs.\nSpecifically, we employ a GAT as the encoder and explore the influence of depth and width. And experiments are conducted on ogbn-Products of around 2 million nodes. The results are shown in Figure[3](#S3.F3 \"Figure 3 ‣ 3.3. Ablation Studies ‣ 3. Doubling the hidden size leads to a performance improvement of nearly 2% in accuracy when the hidden size does not exceed 1024.\nBut further enlarging the width only brings very marginal gain.\n\nAnother way to increase the capacity is to stack more network layers.\nFigure[3](#S3.F3 \"Figure 3 ‣ 3.3. Ablation Studies ‣ 3. Experiments ‣ GraphMAE2: A Decoding-enhanced Masked Self-supervised Graph Learner\") shows that increasing the depth can slightly boost the performance as the accuracy increases by 0.65% when the number of network layers is increased from 2 to 4. And the benefits would diminish when stacking more layers. One possible reason is that deeper GNNs are harder to optimize, while current downstream tasks or semantics of homogeneous structured data benefit little from more complex network architecture.\nIt is observed that the influence of the depth is less remarkable than the width of GNN.\n\n<img src='x3.png' alt='Refer to caption' title='' width='207' height='62' />\n\n<img src='x4.png' alt='Refer to caption' title='' width='216' height='65' />\n\n*Figure 3.
